Under Two Flags

Measuring Racial Bias in Sports Journalism

written by Kyara Lucas | Aug. 2017

As Curaçao is a member of the Dutch Kingdom, Curaçao born athletes usually compete under the Dutch flag. Recently I’ve seen a lot of posts on my Facebook feed adressing the fact that whenever a Curaçao born athlete performs well, the Dutch media refers to them as Dutch; however when they perform badly, they’re refered to as Curaçaoan. I’m going to test this hypothesis with data.



The dataset

I collected all articles written in 2017 on NOS.nl, NU.nl and Telegraaf.nl about the top 5 most popular Curaçaoan athletes, namely: Churandy Martina, Jurickson Profar, Jean-Julien Rojer, Kenley Jansen and Jair Jurrjens. Next, I separate the data set into two subsets. I differentiate between articles where the athlete is referred to as Dutch, and articles where they’re referred to as Curaçaoan.



Measuring performance

I know a positive article when I read one, but translating that intuition into a number isn’t easy. To assess whether the article is about a “positive performance” by the athlete, I conduct a “sentiment analysis”. A sentiment analysis (a.k.a. opinion mining) uses natural language processing to assess and quantify the ‘attitude’ of a text. This algorithm assigns a number between -1 and 1 to each article, negative numbers signifying a “negative performance” and a positive number signifying a “positive performance” (obviously). If the result of the sentiment for a given article is equal to zero, then this is a "neutral" article.



A few results of the sentiment analysis

The article "Martina richt zich op volgend seizoen" got a score of 0. The article titled "Pijnlijke hamstring kost Martina WK atletiek" received a score of -0.2. The article titled "Haase en Rojer zetten Nederland op voorsprong" got a score of 0.2.

Conclusion

I do not find a significant difference in sentiment scores when comparing the Dutch articles, to the Curaçaoan articles. Furthermore, I do not find a significant correlation between the negative articles and the Curaçaoan articles. Thus, I conclude that there is not enough evidence to back-up the claims of racial bias in sports journalism for Curaçao born athletes. This doesn’t mean that it doesn’t happen sometimes, it just means that there’s no statistical evidence to prove that it happens on a systematic basis.



Limitations

It is important to note the limitations of this study. Firstly, the data set is rather small as it consists of less than 30 articles written in 2017. To test the difference between our subgroups we use parametric statistical methods which rely on normal approximation, which traditionally require a sample size which is greater than or equal to 30.
