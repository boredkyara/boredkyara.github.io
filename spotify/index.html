Who Has the Most Boring Music Taste?

An Analysis of Spotify Data

written by Kyara Lucas | Jan. 2018

I’ve been playing around a lot with Spotify’s API service. I found out that Spotify actually keeps track of the “audio features” of all the tracks in a given playlist. These audio features represent characteristics about a song, such as how danceable and loud it is. So, I decided it would be a fun little experiment to analyse the playlists of my friends, more specifically I will use these audio features to assess who has the most boring taste.



Spotify Audio Features

Spotify’s audio features are variables assigned to each song to assess certain characteristics of the song. The value of each feature ranges between 0 and 1. Here’s a short description of each audio feature:

Valence

A measure from 0 to 1 describing the musical positiveness conveyed by a track.


Danceability

Describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical elements


Energy

Represents a perceptual measure of intensity and activity. Typically, energetic tracks feel fast, loud, and noisy.

Speechiness

Detects the presence of spoken words in a track.


Acousticness

This value describes how acoustic a song is. A score of 1.0 means the song is most likely to be an acoustic one.

Instrumentalness

This value represents the amount of vocals in the song. The closer it is to 1.0, the more instrumental the song is.

For example, we can look at the audio features of the classic “Work” by Rihanna ft. Drake, a popular club anthem. Work scored a 0.725 on dance-ability, and a 0.296 on valence. In comparison, the winner of the 2017 Eurovision song contest “Amar Pelos Dois” by Salvador Sobral, a jazz waltz about lost love, scored a 0.21 on danceability and 0.167 on valence.

How to measure boringness?

The most fun part of this analysis is constructing a variable to measure boringness, because, what exactly is a boring song? A boring song for me could be the most lit song ever for one of my friends. Here, I use an equation proposed by data blogger Juan De Dios Santos. The way Juan approached this problem was by imagining himself at a party, and thinking of what kind of music he would like to hear at said party. Juan came up with a simple equation that involves the energy, danceability and loudness of a song. The reason why he chose these is because at a party one would like to have loud music, with a nice dance tempo that brings out the energy to get in the mood for dancing. This is the equation:


boringness = loudness + (energy⋅100) + (danceability⋅100)


We multiply energy and danceability by 100 such that all variables are in the same range. According to this definition, the lower the value the more boring a song is, and the higher the score, the more fun. The average boringness scores are summarised in the following graph:
